# Craft levers

Removing AI tells gets you to "not obviously a robot." It does not get you to "good." This file is the difference. Pull it when the goal is writing that actually pulls the reader forward, not just prose that passes a scan.

The throughline: **write like you are explaining something to a smart, skeptical friend.** That friend will not sit through throat-clearing, will not believe a vague claim, and will tune out the second you sound like a brochure. Every lever below serves that one stance.

These pair naturally with the rhythm and voice work in the main workflow. Use them when you have a draft that is clean but flat.

## 1. The headline does most of the work

For anything with a title, headline, subject line, or hook, the first line carries the piece. Write at least five before you pick one, because the best one usually beats the others by a wide margin and you cannot find it by writing one and stopping.

A strong formula: **action verb + specific outcome + a timeframe or a contrast.**

- Weak: "Improving your launch process"
- Strong: "Ship your startup in days, not weeks"
- Weak: "Tips for better sleep"
- Strong: "Fall asleep in 10 minutes without melatonin"

Specific and concrete beats clever and vague almost every time.

## 2. The opening line has one job: earn the second line

Not to summarize. Not to set context. Just to make the reader keep going. Four openings that work:

- **A direct challenge:** "You are backing up the wrong files."
- **A small scene:** "It's 2am and the deploy is still red."
- **A specific result:** "We cut onboarding from nine days to two. Here is the part that actually mattered."
- **A confession:** "I shipped this bug to 40,000 people before I noticed."

Never open with "In today's fast-paced world" or "Throughout history" or any sweeping windup. That is the single most common AI opener and it makes a reader's eyes slide off the page.

## 3. Quantify the pain

Vague problems feel unsolvable, so readers do nothing. Specific, counted problems feel fixable. Do the arithmetic for them.

- Vague: "Launching is a lot of work."
- Quantified: "Four hours on the emails. Six on the landing page. Another twelve chasing copy and screenshots. That is most of a work-week gone before you have a single user."

Numbers create a gap the reader can feel, and a gap is what makes them want the rest of the piece.

## 4. The "so what" chain

This is where most writing dies: it states a feature and stops. Push every claim down until it hits something a person actually feels, usually emotional or financial. Go about three levels.

> Fast database
> → queries load instantly
> → users don't bounce
> → you stop waking up at 3am worried about churn

Then write from the bottom of the chain, not the top. "Your database is fast" is a spec. "You stop waking up worried about churn" is a reason to care. The bottom rung is almost always the sentence worth keeping.

## 5. Open loops

Tease something without resolving it yet, so the reader has to keep going to close the loop. "But that is not even the worst part." "I will get to why that backfired." Used well, these are the engine of a long piece.

Two to four per page. More than that and it reads as manipulative and cheap, which is its own kind of tell.

## 6. Rhythm is the giveaway, in both directions

Same point as burstiness, said for craft. Real writing alternates. A short, blunt sentence. Then a longer one that takes its time, adds a clause, circles the idea, and lands somewhere slightly unexpected. Then maybe a fragment.

Same-length sentences and same-length paragraphs are the loudest signal that a machine wrote it. When you finish a draft, scan the shape of the paragraphs on the page. If they are all the same rectangle, break them up.

## 7. Testimonials and quotes: five parts

If the piece uses social proof, a flat "Great product!" is worthless and reads as invented. A real-sounding one has: the **before** state, the **action** they took, a **specific outcome**, a **timeframe**, and an **emotion**.

- Weak: "This tool is amazing!"
- Strong: "I was rewriting the same three support replies forty times a day. I wired this up on a Tuesday afternoon. By Friday it was handling 80% of them and I almost cried opening the inbox."

The specificity is what makes it believable. Vague praise reads as fake to a skeptical friend, and your reader is one.

## 8. The avoid list and the read-aloud test

Cut on sight: "delve," "utilize," "leverage" as a verb, "game-changer," "in today's world," "let's dive in," and a scatter of em dashes faking rhythm (see `ai-tells.md` for the full set).

Then the final test, the one that catches everything else: read it out loud. If it sounds like a textbook, a press release, or a chatbot being helpful, it is not done. Rewrite until it sounds like a person who knows the subject talking to someone they respect.

The fundamentals of good writing have not changed in a hundred years. The only new requirement is that it has to sound like a person wrote it, because now most of it doesn't.
